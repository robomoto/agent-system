# Status and Reputation Dynamics

## Social Comparison Theory (Festinger, 1954)

People evaluate their own opinions and abilities by comparing themselves to others. This happens automatically and can't be fully suppressed.

**In community design:**
- Any visible metric (post count, reactions, badges) becomes a basis for social comparison
- Upward comparison ("they post so much more than me") reduces participation motivation
- Downward comparison ("at least I contribute more than them") creates hierarchy
- In small communities, comparison is personal: "Alice posts 3x more than me" not "power users post more"

**Design principle:** Minimize quantified comparison surfaces. Show enough to signal activity, not enough to rank members.

## Status Hierarchies in Small Groups

All groups develop informal status hierarchies. The question isn't whether hierarchy exists, but whether the platform amplifies or moderates it.

**Organic hierarchy signals (always present):**
- Posting frequency (visible in thread activity)
- Quality of contributions (visible through responses and engagement)
- Social connections (visible through reply patterns)
- Founding member status (visible through join date)

**Platform-amplified signals (design choices):**
- Post counts on profiles
- "Top contributor" badges
- Reaction counts
- Admin/moderator badges
- "Member since" dates

**Recommendations for small communities:**
- Avoid leaderboards, point systems, or explicit ranking
- Moderator status should be visible (transparency) but not elevated (status)
- Don't show post counts on profiles — it discourages new members
- If showing join date, frame it as history ("Joined March 2026") not status ("Founding member")

## The Reputation Economy

In neighborhood communities, online reputation blends with real-world reputation:
- A negative interaction online affects how neighbors treat each other in person
- Reputation is not reset — it accumulates across all channels and time
- "Reputation bankruptcy" (creating a new account) isn't possible when your identity is tied to your address

**Implications:**
- Features that permanently mark someone (badges for bad behavior, visible warning counts) are disproportionately harmful
- Allow redemption: people change, and in a neighborhood they can't leave
- Moderation history should not be publicly visible
- Focus on behavior, not person: "This post was removed" not "This user is problematic"

## Newcomer Integration

New members face "legitimate peripheral participation" (Lave & Wenger, 1991) — they start at the edges and gradually move toward full participation.

**Barriers to integration:**
- Established in-jokes and references they don't understand
- Dense discussion threads that feel impenetrable
- Visible post-count gaps signaling their outsider status
- Existing relationship networks they're not part of

**Design support:**
- Welcome thread or introductions channel with low barrier to first post
- New member highlighting (brief, optional) — "Wave to Chris who just joined!"
- Avoid showing total post counts or "tenure" prominently
- Ensure new members can find and join active conversations easily
- The invitation model helps: they already know someone, reducing newcomer anxiety
