# Prosocial Design Patterns

## Reciprocity (Cialdini, 2001)

People feel obligated to return favors. This is the most reliable social mechanism for building community engagement.

**Applications:**
- Welcome messages from existing members (creates reciprocity obligation to participate)
- "First post" celebrations or acknowledgments
- Notification design: showing that someone responded to YOUR post (personal reciprocity > broadcast)
- Digest emails frame the community as giving to you, creating obligation to give back

**Anti-pattern:** Gamification that replaces genuine reciprocity with points. "You earned 10 community points!" feels transactional, not social.

## Social Proof (Cialdini, 2001)

People look to others' behavior to determine appropriate action, especially in ambiguous situations.

**Applications:**
- Showing activity indicators ("15 new posts today") signals the community is alive and participation is normal
- Showing who posted (not just what) leverages personal social proof in small groups
- New member onboarding: seeing familiar neighbors already active lowers participation anxiety

**Anti-pattern:** Showing low activity counts when the community is quiet. "0 new posts" signals abandonment. Better to hide counts when low and show them when high.

## Cooperative Framing (Deutsch, 1949)

How a situation is framed — competitive vs. cooperative — dramatically affects behavior. The same feature can be prosocial or antisocial depending on framing.

**Applications:**
- Channel descriptions should use cooperative language: "Share", "Help", "Discuss" not "Post", "Rate", "Rank"
- Event RSVPs framed as "Who's coming?" (cooperative) vs. "Are you going?" (individual decision)
- Moderation framed as community health, not punishment: "This helps keep conversations productive" vs. "You violated rule #3"

## Commitment and Consistency (Cialdini, 2001)

Once people make a small commitment, they're more likely to follow through with larger related actions.

**Applications:**
- The invitation model is excellent for this: being invited by a neighbor is an implicit commitment to participate
- Having users choose channel subscriptions during onboarding creates commitment to those channels
- Asking for profile details (display name, bio) during signup increases investment in the community

## Sense of Community (McMillan & Chavis, 1986)

Four factors that create psychological sense of community:
1. **Membership**: boundaries, emotional safety, personal investment, shared symbol system
2. **Influence**: members feel they matter to the group, and the group matters to members
3. **Integration**: members' needs are met through community membership
4. **Shared emotional connection**: shared history, positive interaction, spiritual bond

**For design:**
- Membership: invite-only model naturally creates this. Don't weaken the boundary.
- Influence: features that let members shape the community (create channels, organize events) increase sense of community. Read-only members feel less connected.
- Integration: the community must be practically useful (event info, neighbor connections), not just social
- Shared connection: community history features (milestones, "this day last year") build emotional bonds
