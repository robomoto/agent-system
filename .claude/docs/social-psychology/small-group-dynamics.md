# Small Group Dynamics (N < 200)

## Dunbar's Number and Community Layers

Robin Dunbar's social brain hypothesis identifies natural group-size layers:
- **5**: intimate support group (emotional closeness)
- **15**: sympathy group (you'd be upset if they died)
- **50**: close group (regular social contact)
- **150**: Dunbar's number (you can maintain a personal relationship)
- **500**: acquaintance layer (you recognize them and know their name)

**Implications for ~100-member communities:**
- Everyone is within "Dunbar's number" — they can know each other personally
- Social actions are personal, not anonymous. Blocking someone means blocking a neighbor.
- Reputational effects are strong: behavior in the community is visible to everyone
- Free-riding is noticeable and self-correcting at this scale (unlike large platforms)
- Clique formation is the primary social risk, not anonymity-driven toxicity

## Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)

People derive self-esteem from group memberships. Even minimal group distinctions ("red team vs. blue team") create in-group favoritism and out-group bias.

**For community design:**
- Any feature that creates subgroups (private channels, interest groups) risks triggering in-group/out-group dynamics
- The effect is stronger in small groups where the subgroups are visible
- Mitigation: ensure most community activity happens in shared spaces; subgroups should supplement, not replace, the main community
- Cross-cutting ties (people in multiple subgroups) reduce intergroup tension

## Group Polarization (Moscovici & Zavalloni, 1969)

Groups tend to make more extreme decisions than individuals. Discussion among like-minded people amplifies existing tendencies.

**For community design:**
- Topic-specific channels can create echo chambers where moderate positions disappear
- Private channels amplify this: without outside voices, group positions drift toward extremes
- Mitigation: cross-posting summaries, encouraging participation in multiple channels

## Social Loafing (Latane et al., 1979)

Individual effort decreases as group size increases, because individual contributions feel less identifiable and less essential.

**For community design:**
- In a 100-person group, diffusion of responsibility is real: "someone else will respond"
- Direct notifications ("Chris posted in your subscribed channel") combat loafing by making requests personal
- Smaller channels/subgroups increase felt responsibility per member
- Visible contribution counts can help but risk creating status anxiety (see status-signaling.md)

## Bystander Effect in Online Communities

The larger the audience for a request, the less likely any individual is to respond. In small communities this is partially mitigated by personal relationships, but channel size still matters.

**For community design:**
- A "general" channel with all 100 members will get fewer responses per-post than a 15-person interest channel
- @ mentions or direct questions to specific people dramatically increase response rates
- Threading helps: a reply feels like a conversation, not a performance for 100 people
